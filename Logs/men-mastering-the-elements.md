## Backdraft, 1991 / The Perfect Storm, 2000

_[Backdraft ](https://youtu.be/DlXcUyFjNgA) is a film about Chicago firefighters upholding the traditional values of their work. The two heroes are Kurt Russell (big bro) and William Baldwin (little bro) who are on the trail of a serial arsonist. [The Perfect Storm](https://youtu.be/RZvljNvnd18) is about a commercial fishing vessel lost at sea to extraordinary weather conditions, starring George Clooney and Mark Wahlberg in comparable big bro/little bro positions._

In a lot of Hollywood action/thriller movies, female characters offer handy intrusions into what are largely narratives about male relationships, communities, and experiences. The difference, demands and desires female characters present supports the articulation of a hyperbolic, somewhat fantastical, perhaps idealised, maybe just conservative masculinity. At a time of crisis for white hetero men, which is having serious political implications the left has a tendency to undermine as mere stupidity, I am increasingly interested in what it is about "being a man" that has eroded. How is the normative masc societal position deemed at threat - expressed through male, female and self relations - narrativised?

In Backdraft and The Perfect Storm, the respective elements of fire and water offer a useful narrative device for exploring the pursuit of some masculine ideal of freedom, and the complications of being a trad man. We occupy the male characters' perspective, a thrill-seeker (a true man can never be tamed, can never settle), who somehow maintains a fraught but monogamous relationship with a woman (who normatively desires that her man settle down with her), while also retaining a deep loyalty to his bros (they understand his primal desire to thrill-seek like no woman ever could). Maintaining and losing the balance between these three relations drives these films forward. If we see the elements as the object that satisfies the urge to thrill-seek, pursuing them distances the male characters from their women, and unites the bros.

In both these films, the interplay of relations is relayed through the mass media of the time, and through the male character's immediate experience manoeuvring the vast unconquerable sea and thwarting the wily ways of fire. On the one hand, witnessing tragedy at a distance through mass media is possibly the more familiar position for western audiences. On the other hand, the unfamiliar immediate experience of brute determination against the odds is far more exciting. While we follow our masculine men enduring treacherous yet thrilling situations, we also see his woman safely waiting with fraught devotion for her hero to return. In the Perfect Storm, Matt Damon's lady sits in a small town bar and watches the TV with other women who have accepted man's primal need to be facing death as often, and as profitably, as possible. In Backdraft, there's a photographer on scene for the particularly tragic firefighting incidents who takes a familiar Nat Geo trauma-porn photo that then circulates across papers, magazines and TV screens. The feminine is the distanced, familiar position for me and most people who have the privilege of not enduring bodily harm, or potential death, on a day-to-day basis. The other position follows men fighting fire and dodging waves: guided by a sage father figure cultivating his junior's elemental instincts and set against the familiarity of all-male teams, which ensures conflict is largely reduced to throwing punches. The immediacy of this action-packed masculine might is a fun position for any viewer to occupy. Nonetheless, given the continual risks to life and the largely uninterrupted homogeneity, it is an impossible position to sustain - which is why both of these films end in tragedy.

The Perfect Storm retains a respectful reverence that seems reasonable considering the true story it's based on. The narrativisation of masculinity is limited by a conservative premise: fishing at sea is a traditionally male profession, and these labourers have the responsibility of earning money for their women and children. Only Captain Clooney has relinquished any hope of settling down, his tortured freedom from responsibility driving him to wholly dedicate himself to the pursuit of mastery over the elements. Without the drama of navigating difference, this single-minded derangement ultimately undermines the wisdom of his seniority.

Backdraft benefits from being entirely fictional, and revels in the excesses that are limited to and by George Clooney's character. It features a sex scene directly cutting to the foreplay of finding a fire. There's the Heat-esque threat of crossing over to the dark side, as the two types of men who love to play with fire are psychos and firemen - the only ones who understand what it really means to be a male man that loves fire. This macho psychosis gets in the way of Kurt Russell's hetero romance, but only fuels his drive to protect his little brother (William Baldwin), who is still green to dancing with death. Bobby Deniro's been burned before and offers Baby Baldwin a second, contrasting father figure who is wise, patient and without a personal life. Archetypal characterisations across a layered narrative present various men who each manage their unstoppable urge to control the inherent danger of fire differently. As the film remains in society, rather than out at sea, there are more impositions on the pursuit of the primal masculine fantasy. Primarily, the omnipresence of budget cuts, which threatens the sanctity of their work without mention of possible threats the general public - these men will push themselves ever closer to death to protect, so it is only they who are at risk. 

What I take from these films is the crushing weight of attempting to attain a fantastical yet still conservative vision of some primal yet noble masculine freedom, which can never be achieved without the increased likelihood of death. This pursuit is contextualised and foregrounded by men belonging to an all-male team participating in traditionally male jobs. At a remove, female characters worry for their fella's safety (doting mother/madonna), while the threateningly proximate natural elements are disinterested and unrelenting (the hypothetical possibility of endless female orgasm/whore). Caught in the middle are men learning to master the elements, labouring men sharing the perils of responsibility and navigating the multiplicity of desire. The appeal of this untenable masculinity and the price paid in it's pursuit is resolved into a terribly thrilling, entertaining and emotionally unambiguous set of Hollywood productions.
